# Adult mortality gradients: the evidence base

Adult mortality by household wealth **cannot come from DHS-type surveys**: to know
a dead adult's wealth you must have observed their household before they died.
Cross-sections can't (the dead aren't interviewed), and the sibling-survival
method (how DHS measures adult mortality) carries no wealth data for the deceased
sibling's household. That is why the world's evidence splits: **children by
wealth** (the mother's household is observed), **adults by education** (fixed
early in life, recorded at death) — and **adults by wealth only in panels and
surveillance sites**, which observe people before death.

This memo collects the quantitative evidence, by design type, as of July 2026.
All sources are open access.

## Panel studies (nationally representative, wealth-based)

**South Africa — Ardington & Gasealahwe (NIDS waves 1–2, 2008–2010).**
Each additional household asset associated with **5% lower odds of dying** over
the two-year follow-up (n ≈ 21,000; controls: age, sex, population group, urban,
household size, log expenditure — the expenditure control means this understates
the total wealth gradient). Deceased averaged 5.79 household assets vs 6.72 among
survivors (p < 0.001). The only estimate found anywhere that is simultaneously
national, wealth-based, and open.
*Mortality in South Africa: Socio-economic profile and association with
self-reported health*, Development Southern Africa 31(1), 2014. Open manuscript:
https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3890238/

**South Africa — education-based national benchmark (NIDS 2008–2017).**
Remaining life expectancy at age 20: gap of **11.1 years (men) / 9.3 years
(women)** between the lowest (below secondary) and highest (post-secondary) of
three education groups.
*Multimorbid life expectancy across race, socio-economic status, and sex in South
Africa*, Population Studies, 2024.
https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11956785/

## Surveillance sites (wealth-based, subnational)

**South Africa — Agincourt HDSS (rural Mpumalanga, 2001–2013, >1m person-years).**
Probability of dying between 15 and 60, poorest vs richest household wealth
quintile: **men 440 vs 323 per 1,000** (2008–10); women 326 vs 265 (2011–13, gap
no longer significant by then). Under-5: 43 vs 19 per 1,000 (2011–13). HIV/TB
mortality concentrates in the poorest quintiles despite free ART.
*Lancet Global Health*, 2017. https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5559644/

## Global meta-analytic anchors

**Education–mortality dose-response (IHME/CHAIN, 603 studies, 59 countries).**
**1.9% lower all-cause mortality hazard per additional year of education**
overall; age-varying: **2.9%/year at ages 18–49, 0.8%/year over 70**; no
detectable difference by sex or country development level. Useful for the *age
shape* of any adult gradient even when the level is set from wealth-based
sources.
*Effects of education on adult mortality: a global systematic review and
meta-analysis*, Lancet Public Health, 2024.
https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10901745/ — estimates downloadable from
IHME GHDx.

**Wealth–mortality in rich-country panels.** The wealth gradient is confirmed in
US/European panel data (NEJM 2025 US–Europe comparison; JAMA 2018 negative-wealth-
shock study: adjusted mortality HR 1.50 after a wealth loss), and systematic
reviews find **wealth a stronger mortality predictor than income or education** —
supporting wealth as the dimension of choice where it can be measured.

**Registry studies by measured income (method precedent, rich countries only).**
US tax records (Chetty et al., JAMA 2016 — life expectancy by income percentile,
estimates public), Norway (Kinge et al., JAMA 2019), Sweden (PNAS 2025). These
document monotonic income-percentile gradients, steepest at the bottom of the
distribution — relevant to functional-form choices when extrapolating any
gradient to top income groups.

**A caution.** Gradients are country-specific: Costa Rica is the documented case
of a near-absent adult SES–mortality gradient. Borrow levels across countries
with care; anchor on national evidence wherever any exists.

## Middle-income linked-registry frontier

**Brazil — the 100 Million Cohort (CIDACS).** ~45 million people from the social-
program registry (with measured income) linked to national death records,
2011–2018 — the only LMIC infrastructure comparable to rich-country registry
studies. Published gradients so far are domain-specific (cash-transfer mortality
effects, infectious disease SES gradients); coverage is the low-income segment of
the population. Watch this cohort for an all-cause income-gradient estimate.
